The Planets

The "ExoWorlds" list of exoplanets being available for public naming at the initiative of the IAU is a list compiled from several exoplanet databases, including exoplanet.eu (Schneider et al. 2011) and exoplanets.org (Wright at al. 2011).

This list includes well-studied exoplanets discovered over twenty years, up to Dec.31, 2008. A time of at least five years since the discovery has been considered as a simple and good criterion to include exoplanets, which can be considered as confirmed. The exoplanets in the list have a minimum mass below 13 times the Jupiter mass. All the discoveries were made using various methods, including radial velocities, transits, microlensing and direct imagery.

For these exoplanets, the scientific nomenclature follows the nomenclature rules widely adopted by the scientific community, which are drawn from the rules for naming binary stars. For each planet, the name of the host star (around which planets are orbiting) is followed by a lower-case letter: b for the first discovered exoplanet, c for the second, etc. (The letters would be capitalized in the case of binaries: the "primary" star name is followed by "A", and its companion stars are labeled by the same name followed by "B", "C", etc.).

In the ExoWorlds list, two stars already have common names: Fomalhaut (alpha Piscis Austrinis) is one of the four "royal stars" of ancient Persia, with Albebaran, Antares, and Regulus; Pollux (beta Geminorum) is the twin brother of Castor, son of Zeus (Jupiter) and Leda, from the ancient Greek and Roman mythologies -the constellation "Gemini" is named after them ("Gemini" means "twins" in latin). So these stars cannot be considered for public naming.

Rules and Privacy

What will be named?

  1. A list of 305 well-characterised exoplanets, discovered prior to 31 December 2008. The date refers to the date of submission to a refereed journal. Many exoplanets discovered after this date require confirmation or are incompletely characterised.
  2. These exoplanets belong to 260 exoplanetary systems comprising one to five members, in addition to the host star, which can also be named.
  3. During the NameExoWorlds campaign, proposals for names for the selected systems and components shall be made only via the campaign process.
  4. The systems have been selected for naming by the IAU Working Group Exoplanets for the Public, and are published on the www.NameExoWorlds.org website.

Who can submit names?

  1. Only astronomy clubs or non-profit, cultural organisations with a proven interest in astronomy, (hereafter "organisations" for short) based in any country, shall be allowed to propose names.
  2. To suggest names, organisations shall register on the IAU Directory for World Astronomy website providing their web site URL, the organisation’s registration number/certificate/document number testifying its status, and the full name, e-mail and postal address of a contact person.
  3. The organisation shall have a web site.
  4. The web site of the organisation shall have a demonstrated activity or interest in astronomy, and a verifiable non-profit status.

How can names be submitted?

  1. Organisations may send only one proposal, concerning only one of the selected systems, irrespective of the number of exoplanets belonging to the system.
    • The process is open equally to everyone
    • Organisations do not gather suggestions for names by means of sales, donations or other financial transactions
    • Organisations do not sell their right to suggest names to other entities or try to gain any other non-commercial or commercial benefit from their right to suggest names to the IAU.
    • A web page of the campaign is set up and a formal letter sent to IAU (to email) before launch. The IAU shall play only an advisory role in any eventual local campaigns.
    • The process respects the IAU Exoplanet Naming Theme rules.
    • The process follows the guide for organising such campaigns: http://iau.org/public/themes/naming_exoplanets/
  2. Following a local cmapign or not, the organisations may then propose names for the selected systems to the IAU, following the naming rules in the IAU Exoplanet Naming Theme.
  3. Organisations shall write a justification in English (max. 1 page or 500 words).
  4. Organisations shall submit a statement that the suggested names are free for public use, and that the organisations transfers any intellectual property rights to the IAU

What names can be submitted?

  1. Proposed names should be:
    • 16 characters or less in length.
    • Preferably one word.
    • Pronounceable (in some language)
    • Non-offensive
    • Not too similar to an existing name of an astronomical object. Names already assigned to astronomical objects can be checked using the links http://cds.u-strasbg.fr/cgi-bin/sesame (for galactic and extragalactic names), and the MPC database http://www.minorplanetcenter.net/db_search (for names).
  2. In addition it is not allowed to propose:
    • Names of pet animals
    • Names of a purely or principally commercial nature;
    • Names of individuals, places or events principally known for political, military or religious activities;
    • Names of living individuals.
  3. Only names that are not protected by trademarks or other forms of intellectual property claims may be proposed
  4. The decision of the IAU, via its Executive Committee Working Group on Public Naming of Planets and Planetary Satellites, on the names to be sent for public vote is done based on the IAU Exoplanet Naming Theme and it is final.
  5. It is understood that, if a scientific designation for the object(s) already exists, the public name will not replace it, but will be recognised by the IAU as the appropriate publicly used name for the object(s), and be publicised as such, along with due credit to the organisation that proposed it. This public name may then be used internationally along with, or instead of, the scientific designation, permanently and without restrictions.

Who can vote?

  1. Anyone can cast a vote on the suggested names, only once for each name
  2. Only participants above the age of 18 may participate
  3. Members of the IAU Executive Committee Working Group on Public Naming of Planets and Planetary Satellites are ineligible for participation

The Process

  • June 2014: a list of 305 well-characterized exoplanets, discovered prior to 31 December 2008 [1], is selected for naming by the IAU Working Group Exoplanets for the Public, and is published on the www.NameExoWorlds.org website. These exoplanets belong to 260 exoplanetary systems comprising one to five members, in addition to the host star.
  • August 2014: an IAU Directory for World Astronomy website (directory.iau.org) allows astronomy clubs and non-profit organisations interested in naming these exoplanets to register. The IAU will have the capability to handle the registration of thousands of such groups. Registration is compulsory.
  • October 2014: clubs or non-profit organisations vote for the 20–30 top exoplanetary systems they wish to name out of the list provided by the IAU. The actual number will depend on how many groups have registered.
  • December 2014: clubs or non-profit organisations send in proposals for the names of members of these selected systems (including the host star), based on the rules in the IAU Exoplanet Naming Theme, together with a detailed supporting argument for their choice. Each group is allowed to name only one exoplanetary system. More details on this stage will be given later.
  • March 2015: the general public votes to rank the proposed names. The IAU and Zooniverse will be ready to handle a million votes or more worldwide.
  • July 2015: the IAU, via its Executive Committee Working Group on the Public Naming of Planets and Planetary Satellites, oversees the final stages of the contest, and validates the winning names from the vote.
  • 3–14 August 2015: the results are announced at a special public ceremony held during the IAU XXIX General Assembly in Honolulu, USA.
